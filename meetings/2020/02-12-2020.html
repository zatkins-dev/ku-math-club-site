February 12, 2020 — KU Math Club Meeting

Meeting Summary

Our 2nd KU Math Club meeting on February 12, 2020 hosted Professor Margaret Bayer who gave a talk on the combinatorial study of polyhedra — three-dimensional geometric objects with flat polygonal faces (think cubes, pyramids, d20 dice, and the like). We can put some photos from the meeting here.

Polyhedra have associated with them three basic variants: a number of “points” or vertices (denoted |V|), a number of edges (denoted |E|), and a number of faces (denoted |F|). Euler’s Polyhedral Formula tells us that

|V| − |E| + |F| = 2,  i.e.,

for every convex polyhedra the number of vertices, minus the number of edges, plus the number of faces, is equal to two.

Of course, many combinations of |V|, |E|, and |F| satisfy this equality, such as |V| = 2 and |E| = |F| = 0, and many, like this one, do not correspond to any polyhedron. Generally, though, this is not so easy to see. The question is: given any arbitrary |V|, |E| and |F|, does any corresponding polyhedra exist? As it turns out, we actually have a set of constraints that, taken together, form necessary and sufficient conditions by which a (|V|, |E|, |F|) triple may correspond to a polyhedron. These constraints are due to Steinitz, 1906:

  • |V| ≥ 4
  • |F| ≥ 4
  • 2|E| ≥ 3|v|
  • 2|E| ≥ 3|f|

Note that these constraints are not independent of each other (i.e., it is possible to derive some of them from the others). So, the (|V|,|E|, |F|) triple of any polyhedron P satisfies the above inequalities, and, conversely, any (|V|, |E|, |F|) triple which satisfies the above inequalities corresponds to some polyhedron. Pretty neat!

Following this, Professor Bayer presented a yet-unsolved problem in the field.

Problem: Let P be a polyhedron and denote by pn(P) the number of faces on P that are n-sided polygons, e.g., for a square-base pyramid S, p3(S) = 4 and p4(S) = 1 .
Given a sequence (p3, p4, p5, …), does there exist a polyhedron with these pn values?

Solution: Generally, there is no answer (yet!). Interestingly, though, we do have a constraint on such sequences with p6 missing, which is also due to Steinitz:
Given non-negative integers p3, p4, p5, p7, p8,..., if the following inequality holds

3p3 + 2p4 + p5 ≥ 12 + p7 + 2p8 + 3p9 + ...,

then there exists some p6 for which the sequence (p3, p4, p5, p6, p7,...) corresponds to some polyhedron P. Very particular, no doubt, and still not a very simple answer, particular as it is! Given the peculiarity of this single case, it may still be a long time before we will be able to say anything more.
